**US court pauses challenges to CFPB open banking rule**

*The US appeals court handling industry challenges to the CFPB's Section 1033 rule has suspended proceedings while the bureau undertakes a substantial revision, leaving the US open banking framework in limbo.*

The US Court of Appeals for the Sixth Circuit granted a stay requested by plaintiffs, including banking trade groups, after the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (CFPB) indicated it intends to substantially revise the open banking rule finalised under the Biden administration. Plaintiffs have argued that the rule exceeds the CFPB's authority under the Dodd-Frank Act.

The pause effectively freezes the legal battle while the rule is rewritten, meaning compliance deadlines remain uncertain for large US banks that were first in line.

For European firms with US operations or cross-border data-sharing ambitions, the delay widens the gap between the EU's Financial Data Access framework and any comparable US regime.

*Editorial note: This is a material development in the US open banking timeline with direct read-across for European firms operating transatlantically and for policymakers watching implementation risks.*

**EU investor data-sharing proposal extends open finance logic to capital markets**

*An Oxford academic analysis highlights how the European Commission's planned open finance framework would apply data-sharing obligations to investment accounts, not just payments, with implications for wealth platforms and asset managers.*

A post on the Oxford Business Law Blog examines the Commission's intent to extend the open finance framework into investor data-sharing as part of its Digital Finance Strategy. The proposal would allow consumers to share investment portfolio data with authorised third parties, paralleling the account information services model in payments but applied to securities holdings, pension pots and insurance products.

The practical effect would be to enable comparison and switching services for investment products, something that has been technically possible but commercially difficult without standardised data access. For wealth management platforms, robo-advisers and banks with investment arms, this creates both opportunity and competitive exposure. The proposal sits within the broader Financial Data Access regulation, which remains under negotiation, but the direction of travel is now clear: the data-sharing logic that began with payment accounts is being systematically extended across financial services.

*Editorial note: This is an important signal on the scope of EU open finance, moving the conversation beyond payments and banking into capital markets territory that affects a different set of industry players.*
