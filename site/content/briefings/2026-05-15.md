**NAB acquires London pay-by-bank fintech Banked in cross-border deal**

*National Australia Bank's acquisition of UK-based account-to-account payments firm Banked signals major-bank appetite for pay-by-bank capabilities beyond Europe's fintech layer.*

NAB has acquired Banked, a London-headquartered fintech that built an account-to-account payment acceptance platform competing with card networks, for an undisclosed sum. Banked had positioned itself as infrastructure for merchants seeking lower-cost alternatives to card payments, using open banking rails to initiate transactions directly from consumer bank accounts.

A top-four Australian bank buying a UK open banking payments firm outright, rather than partnering or investing, reflects growing confidence that pay-by-bank is mature enough to integrate into mainstream banking propositions. NAB's stated aim is to help its business customers receive payments faster, suggesting the technology will be folded into its merchant services rather than run as a standalone venture.

For the European open banking payments sector, the acquisition is a data point in a broader pattern: established banks moving from observation to ownership. It also raises questions about whether other standalone pay-by-bank firms will be acquired rather than scale independently.

**Bank of England finds "significant trade-offs" in DLT for wholesale settlement**

*After months of experimentation, the Bank of England has concluded that distributed ledger technology presents unresolved tensions between governance, resilience and scalability for wholesale payments.*

The Bank of England has published findings from its programme of distributed ledger technology (DLT) experiments for wholesale settlement. The central bank reports "significant trade-offs" across governance, operational resilience and scalability, and says it remains unclear whether DLT offers net advantages over existing infrastructure for settling wholesale payments.

It is a sobering assessment for tokenisation advocates as the Bank of England is one of several major central banks — alongside the ECB, BIS and others — exploring whether tokenised settlement can modernise post-trade infrastructure. A finding that the technology introduces as many problems as it solves carries weight, particularly as the ECB advances its own DLT settlement trials and the industry debates whether to invest in parallel infrastructure.

The result does not close the door on DLT in wholesale markets, but it provides a credible counterweight to the more optimistic narratives from technology vendors and some market infrastructure operators. For payments professionals, the practical implication is that any move to DLT-based settlement in major European markets is likely to be slower and more cautious than proponents have suggested.

*Editorial note: A major central bank expressing substantive scepticism about DLT for wholesale settlement is a significant signal for European market infrastructure planning.*

**SurePay launches free IBAN verification for Dutch taxpayers**

*The Dutch confirmation-of-payee provider has extended its service to tax payments, illustrating how verification of payee is moving beyond bank-to-bank transfers following the EU Instant Payments Regulation mandate.*

SurePay, the Dutch firm behind the country's IBAN-Name Check service, has launched a free verification tool allowing taxpayers to confirm the identity of government payees before making payments to the Dutch tax authority. The service lets individuals check that an IBAN corresponds to the correct recipient name before initiating a transfer.

The move comes after eurozone banks were required to adopt verification of payee under the EU Instant Payments Regulation. SurePay's extension into government payments shows how the infrastructure built for that mandate is finding applications beyond its original scope. Rather than remaining a fraud-prevention layer within commercial banking, confirmation of payee is becoming a general-purpose trust mechanism across different payment contexts.

For payments professionals, the Dutch example offers a preview of how verification of payee infrastructure may evolve across Europe as adoption matures — moving from a compliance obligation into a broader public utility.

*Editorial note: An early concrete example of verification of payee extending beyond its original mandate scope, relevant as other eurozone markets prepare for implementation.*
